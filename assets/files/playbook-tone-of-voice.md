**PLAYBOOK ADVISORY GROUP LTD**

## 01 — INTRODUCTION

### What this document is for

This guide defines how Playbook Advisory Group communicates in writing — across its website, thought leadership, proposals, emails, and any other client-facing material. It is a working reference for anyone producing content on behalf of the company.

The guide does not describe a style. It describes a discipline. Playbook's brand authority depends on writing that sounds as senior, considered, and independent as the advisory work it represents. Inconsistency in tone undermines the credibility the brand is building.

**Use this document before writing, not after.** It is a check, not a correction.

## 02 — BRAND PERSONALITY

### Our brand personality

- **Authoritative, not imposing.**  
  Playbook holds a strong point of view and expresses it without qualification. Authority comes from depth of insight, not volume of assertion. We do not raise our voice to be heard.

- **Direct, not blunt.**  
  We reach the point quickly and without preamble. We do not soften positions to avoid discomfort — but we are never gratuitously blunt. Every sentence earns its place.

- **Calm, not cold.**  
  Even in high-stakes contexts, the Playbook voice is controlled and measured. We do not perform urgency. Our composure is itself a signal of credibility. Calm is not detachment — it is confidence.

- **Rigorous, not academic.**  
  We think clearly and precisely. The Playbook intellectual platform — the Paradox, the three memory gaps, the model — is genuinely complex, but we express it plainly. Complexity in the ideas; simplicity in the writing.

- **Assured, not arrogant.**  
  We write from a position of confidence earned through experience. We do not seek approval or hedge our conclusions. But we never condescend to the people we are writing for — they are senior, intelligent, and time-poor.

## 03 — VOICE

### The four dimensions of our voice

#### 1. Personality
If Playbook walked into a boardroom, it would be the most senior person there who speaks least but says most. It listens carefully, waits for the right moment, and then offers an observation that reframes the conversation. It does not perform expertise — it simply has it. The room adjusts to it, not the other way around.

> “Every complex programme eventually writes its playbook. The only question is whether it is written before the mistakes or after them.”  
> “Organisations do not lack experience. They lack a way to remember it.”

#### 2. Tone
The baseline Playbook tone is measured and precise. It shifts across contexts — sharper and more declarative in headlines and proposals, slightly warmer in direct client communications — but it never becomes casual or effusive. We do not celebrate, exclaim, or enthuse. We illuminate, challenge, and reassure.

The emotional register we aim for in our reader: **recognition, not surprise. Reassurance, not relief. Confidence, not excitement.**

> “This feels controlled and serious.”  
> “These people understand governance exposure.”

#### 3. Language style
Playbook writes for senior executives who read quickly and remember precisely. Sentences are short. Paragraphs earn their length. Vocabulary is precise but never showy — we use the right word, not the impressive one.

We do not use consulting idioms: no “leveraging capabilities”, no “value-add”, no “stakeholder alignment journeys”. We describe real things in plain terms. Where we use specialist language — governance, institutional memory, sponsor-side — we own it confidently, because we have defined what it means.

We write in **British English** throughout.

#### 4. Point of view
Playbook speaks from the sponsor’s perspective, not the delivery machine’s. We write as a trusted senior adviser — not a vendor selling a product, not a consultant describing a methodology. We address our reader as a peer who recognises the problem we are naming, not as a prospect who needs educating.

Our content opens with the truth of their situation, not with our offer. The offer follows from the argument, not the other way around.

## 04 — STYLE

### How we write

#### Vocabulary and word choice
- Use concrete nouns and active verbs. Avoid nominalisations: “we assessed” not “we conducted an assessment of”.
- Prefer Anglo-Saxon words over Latinate equivalents where they are equally precise: “use” not “utilise”, “show” not “demonstrate”, “help” not “facilitate”.
- Use “sponsor” and “sponsor-side” precisely and consistently — it is a term of art, not a synonym for “client”.
- Use “governance” deliberately — it refers to the structures and decision disciplines that direct programmes, not administration in general.
- Use “judgement” (British spelling) throughout, not “judgment”.
- Numbers one to nine are written as words; 10 and above as numerals, unless at the start of a sentence.

#### Sentence and paragraph length
- Keep most sentences under 20 words. Vary length deliberately — a short sentence after a longer one creates rhythm and emphasis.
- Paragraphs of 3–5 sentences are the standard. Long paragraphs signal either over-explanation or unclear thinking.
- One idea per paragraph in body copy. Do not bury the point in the middle.

#### Punctuation and grammar conventions
- Use the Oxford comma: “clarity, control, and confidence”.
- Em dashes (—) for parenthetical emphasis. Sparingly. Not as a verbal tic.
- Avoid exclamation marks entirely in formal and website copy. One per email if genuinely warranted; none in proposals.
- Semicolons are acceptable; use them to link closely related independent clauses, not as a stylistic flourish.
- Avoid ellipses in professional copy. They imply hesitation we do not have.

#### Capitalisation
- Sentence case for all headings and subheadings, not title case.
- Capitalise named Playbook intellectual concepts: the Playbook Paradox, the Capital Governance Diagnostic, the Memory Gap, the Translation Gap, the Decision Gap, the Governance Bridge.
- “Board” is capitalised when referring to a specific governing body; lower case as a general reference.
- “Programme” — British spelling throughout, not “program” (except in a software context).

#### Numbers, dates, and formatting
- Dates: 26 February 2026 — no ordinals, no abbreviations.
- Currency: £850/day, £1m, £10m — abbreviated for sums above £999.
- Percentages: 25% — numeral plus symbol, no space.
- Avoid bullet point overload in body copy. If the content merits a list, use one; if it can be written as prose, write it as prose.

## 05 — CONTEXT

### What we sound like in context

#### Website — headlines, body, CTAs
Headlines are declarative statements, not questions. They assert a truth the reader already suspects. Body copy moves the reader through an argument: problem → explanation → solution. CTAs are calm invitations, not calls to action dressed as commands.

> “Organisations do not lack experience. They lack a way to remember it.”  
> “The governance bridge between Board oversight and programme delivery.”  
> “Talk to Playbook.” (not “Get in touch today!” or “Book a free consultation”)

#### Proposals and pitch documents
Formal, precise, and confident. Proposals are the brand at its most rigorous. Every claim is grounded. Every recommendation is explained. Tone is senior-to-senior — we are writing for a Director of Estates or a Trust CFO, not a procurement team.

Avoid proposal language that reads as boilerplate: “We are pleased to submit…”, “Playbook is uniquely positioned to…”. Open with the client’s situation; arrive at Playbook’s role as the logical conclusion.

> “The Capital Governance Diagnostic is structured to surface the real issues quickly — and to create the conditions for decisions that hold under scrutiny.”

#### Email
Warmer than website copy, but still restrained. No pleasantries beyond a brief acknowledgement if genuinely appropriate. Get to the point by the second sentence. Sign off simply: “Best regards” or simply the name — not “Kind regards”, not “Many thanks” as a habitual sign-off on substantive correspondence.

> “I wanted to follow up on our conversation about [programme name]. The issues you described — diffuse accountability and a lack of independent challenge at gateway — are exactly the pattern the diagnostic is designed to surface quickly.”

#### Thought leadership — articles, LinkedIn, white papers
This is where the intellectual platform lives. The Playbook Paradox, the three memory gaps, and the concept of institutionalised judgement are the recurring themes. Articles should move through the same narrative arc as the brand: recognition → explanation → model → implication.

LinkedIn posts are shorter, but the same rules apply: no hollow insight, no generic professional advice. Write with a point of view. End with a conclusion, not a question designed to generate comments.

> “The Memory Gap, the Translation Gap, the Decision Gap. These are not abstract failures. They are predictable. Which means they are preventable.”

#### Keynote and presentation copy
Sparse. High contrast. One idea per slide. The spoken voice does the work — slides carry the architecture, not the argument. Use the narrative arc: Reality → Paradox → Failures → Model → Offer. Executives start nodding at the Paradox. Let that do the work.

#### Error messages and operational microcopy
Calm, clear, and functional. No apologies, no exclamation marks. State what has happened and what to do next.

> “This page is unavailable. Return to the homepage or contact us directly.”

## 06 — REFERENCE

### Do / Don’t

| DO                                      | DON’T                                              |
|-----------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|
| Write in declarative, active sentences  | Use passive constructions to hedge conclusions     |
| Name the problem precisely before offering the solution | Lead with Playbook’s credentials before establishing the client’s context |
| Use “sponsor-side” and “governance” as terms of art | Use “stakeholder” as a catch-all for anyone involved in a programme |
| Trust the reader’s intelligence — they are senior executives | Over-explain or define terms they already understand |
| Let the intellectual argument do the credibility work | Use superlatives: “unique”, “world-class”, “market-leading” |
| Write “programme” (British English)     | Write “program” (unless referring to software)     |
| Use sentence case for headings          | Use title case throughout                          |
| End communications with clear, calm conclusions | End with a rhetorical question or an unresolved thought |
| Be specific about what Playbook does and does not do | Use vague advisory language: “we partner with clients to deliver value” |
| Reference named Playbook concepts with confidence | Introduce the Playbook Paradox in every piece without purpose |

#### Proposal opening paragraph

**BEFORE**

The challenge you have described — diffuse accountability, no independent challenge at critical decision points, and escalating costs ahead of financial commitment — follows a recognisable pattern.
