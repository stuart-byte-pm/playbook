The Playbook Paradox
—
"Every complex programme eventually writes its playbook. The only question is whether it is written before the mistakes or after them."

Organisations accumulate vast experience across capital programmes, yet the same problems repeat. The difficulty is rarely a lack of knowledge — knowledge rarely survives long enough to reach the next decision. Playbook exists to close that gap.

Understand the Playbook Paradox
Failures of institutional memory

Experience is lost through three predictable gaps

01

The Memory Gap

Experience remains with individuals rather than the organisation. When people leave, knowledge disappears. The programme moves on; the lesson does not.

02

The Translation Gap

Lessons are recorded but not converted into decision guidance. Knowledge remains descriptive rather than operational — noted, not applied.

03

The Decision Gap

Even when knowledge exists, it rarely reaches the moment decisions are made. Under pressure, organisations rely on instinct rather than experience.

The Playbook model

How experience becomes institutional judgement

Organisations don't lack experience. They lack a way to remember it. The Playbook model describes the four-stage progression that closes that gap.

Explore the model
01
Experience
Experience teaches the lesson. Complex programmes generate the raw material — but only if it is captured before it walks out of the door.
02
Insight
Insight recognises the pattern. Experience becomes insight when organisations can see what recurs — across programmes, decisions, and failures.
03
Judgement
Judgement guides the decision. Insight becomes judgement when patterns are translated into principles that hold under pressure and scrutiny.
04
Institutional memory
A playbook ensures the lesson is not forgotten. Judgement is codified so it survives beyond individuals — and reaches the next decision when the stakes are high.
